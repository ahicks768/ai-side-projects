A large language model is trained by reading enormous amounts of text and repeatedly practicing one task: predicting the next word in a sentence, adjusting itself slightly each time it guesses wrong across billions of examples until it produces remarkably coherent text (a phase often refined further through human feedback, where people rate responses and the model is nudged toward what people actually prefer). To generate a response, the model breaks the input into pieces, gives each one a general learned meaning, and then uses a mechanism called attention to let each piece look at every other piece in the sentence and figure out which words are actually relevant to each other, since a word's true meaning often depends entirely on its surrounding context. Once it has built that contextual understanding, the model produces a ranked list of likely next words, picks one, and repeats the process one word at a time, which is why an LLM is best understood as an extremely sophisticated next word predictor rather than something that understands language the way a person does, even though the results often look strikingly similar.
